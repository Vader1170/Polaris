About Druva

Most students never get taught how to start.

The hardest part is rarely the writing, the experiment, or the argument itself. It's the stretch before any of that, when a vague interest has to become something you can actually sit down and work on. That's what Druva was built to help with.

Why we built this

Talent was never the missing piece. Direction was.

Most students who want to do real independent research, or take on a serious project of their own, hit the same wall. Nobody ever really taught them how to begin: how to pick a question worth spending months on, how to approach a mentor, how to find their footing in a field that looks enormous from the outside.

Schools are set up to teach material with known answers, and they're good at that. The trouble starts the moment a student wants to chase a question nobody has handed them yet. At that point they're mostly on their own, and a lot of them give up. Not because the work is too hard, but because the first step is never made visible.

Druva exists to make that first step visible. It won't do the thinking for you. It will show you the shape of the path clearly enough that you can walk it yourself.

How we think about it

A few things we hold to.

01

The work has to stay yours. We can point at a path and explain why it makes sense, but the thinking, the writing, and the building all need to come from you, or none of it means much.

02

A hypothesis is only as good as the test you run against it. Assumptions feel productive in the moment, but they rarely hold up once you actually check them.

03

Ask for feedback while you're still willing to hear it. Waiting until a project feels finished usually means you've already stopped being open to changing it.

04

Check your own claims before anyone else has to. That habit is what earns a young researcher's work the trust of older ones.

05

You don't need a title, a lab, or a professor's permission to ask a real question. Plenty of good research starts with curiosity and not much else.

06

Understanding the material well enough to defend it matters more than getting it published. The second one tends to follow the first anyway.

The story

Built by someone who needed it first.

Founder

Sourish Balaji Ramakrishnan

Druva started because its founder, Sourish, ran into this exact problem in high school in India, where curiosity was never the issue but structure was. He spent years figuring things out through trial and error: how to turn a scattered interest into a research question, how to make sense of a paper without anyone walking him through it, how to email a professor without knowing the etiquette of the thing.

Once other students started asking him the same questions he used to have no answers to, the pattern got hard to ignore. Clearly this went beyond one person's high school experience; it pointed to a gap in how students are prepared to do independent work at all. Druva is an attempt to turn what he learned the slow way into something a student can use from day one. His own interests stay in math, physics, and independent research, the same ground the tool grew out of.
