About Druva

Most students never get taught how to start.

Not the writing, not the experiment, not the argument — the part before any of that, where you turn a vague interest into something you can actually work on. This is the part Druva exists for.

Why we built this

The gap isn't talent. It's a map.

Most students who want to do real independent research, or build something ambitious of their own, run into the same wall: nobody ever taught them how to begin. Not how to pick a question worth pursuing, not how to find a mentor, not how to get their footing in a field that looks enormous from the outside.

School optimizes for tests with known answers. The moment a student wants to chase a question nobody's handed them yet, they're on their own — and most give up not because the work is too hard, but because the first step is invisible.

Druva exists to make that first step visible. Not by doing the thinking for you, but by showing you the shape of the path so you can walk it yourself.

How we think about it

A few things we hold to.

01

Your work should be your own. We guide the path — the thinking, the writing, and the building stay entirely yours.

02

Evidence beats assumption, every time. A good hypothesis is worth nothing until you've tested it against reality.

03

Feedback early is what makes work good. Seek it out before you're attached to being right.

04

Check your own claims first. The rigor you apply to your own work is the rigor that earns you trust.

05

You don't need a title to ask a real question. Curiosity is the only credential this actually requires.

06

Understanding matters more than publishing. A result you can defend beats a result you can only cite.

The story

Built by someone who needed it first.

Founder

Sourish Balaji Ramakrishnan

Druva started because its founder, Sourish, ran into this exact problem in high school in India. There was no shortage of curiosity — the shortage was structure. He spent years figuring out, largely through trial and error, how to turn scattered interests into research questions, how to read a paper without a guide, and how to approach a mentor without knowing the etiquette of it.

When other students began asking him the same questions he'd once had no answers to, the pattern became obvious: this wasn't a personal problem, it was a structural one. Druva turns what he learned the slow way into a tool anyone can use from day one. His own focus stays in math, physics, and independent research — the same terrain the tool was born out of.
